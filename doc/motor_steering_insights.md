# Handover - Motor Mechanic Steering & Sensor Insights

This document compiles the physical, electrical, and logic design insights for the Paketkasten Revived motor steering and limit switch system. It serves as a comprehensive reference guide for any future agent modifying the firmware.

---

## 1. Physical & Mechanical Layout

The Paketkasten locking mechanism is controlled by a single DC motor driving a central rotating lock rod or cam. As the cam rotates, a single magnet attached to the moving lock mechanism sweeps across three stationary Hall-effect sensors:

$$\text{Closed position} \ (0^\circ) \longrightarrow \text{Parcel position} \ (15^\circ\text{ to }20^\circ) \longrightarrow \text{Mail position} \ (30^\circ)$$

- **`CLOSED_SWITCH_PIN` (GPIO 17)**: Active when the door/latch is fully closed and locked.
- **`PARCEL_SWITCH_PIN` (GPIO 16)**: Active when the latch rotates slightly to unlock the parcel door.
- **`MAIL_SWITCH_PIN` (GPIO 4)**: Active when the latch rotates all the way to unlock the mail slot.
- **Motor Forward (Opening)**: Drives the cam from `Closed` -> `Parcel` -> `Mail`.
- **Motor Reverse (Locking)**: Drives the cam from `Mail`/`Parcel` back to `Closed`.

## 2. Sensor Specifications: Honeywell SL353HT

The board uses **Honeywell SL353HT** Hall-effect ICs for limit detection:
- **Output Type**: **Push-pull output**. They actively drive the lines HIGH (3.3V) and LOW (0V). *Do not rely on external or internal pull-up/pull-down resistors to change their signal states, though pull-ups/pull-downs are useful for stabilizing lines when the sensors are unpowered.*
- **Magnetic Operation**: **Omnipolar** (responsive to either North or South pole).
- **Logic Level**: **Active-low** (drives output LOW when the magnet is near, drives output HIGH when the magnet is removed).
- **Internal Duty Cycling**: The SL353HT is a micropower sensor that runs on an internal duty cycle (13% typical) to save power. It wakes up, samples the magnetic field for $6\mu\text{s}$, updates its latch, and sleeps for the remainder of its $46\mu\text{s}$ period. 
  - *Insight*: The resulting $21.7\text{ kHz}$ sampling frequency is fast enough to ensure it behaves practically as a continuous output. A fast-moving magnet will not slip past the sleep window.

## 3. Solved Engineering Challenges & Architecture

During development, several critical hardware-software integration conflicts were uncovered and resolved:

### A. High-Impedance Floating Line Noise
- **Problem**: Configuring the switch pins as standard `INPUT` left them floating when the microcontroller/board first booted or when lines were unpowered, making them highly susceptible to electromagnetic interference (EMI) generated by the DC motor's brushes.
- **Solution**: Configured switch pins using `INPUT_PULLUP` (or `INPUT_PULLDOWN` if the active state is inverted). This stabilizes the lines and suppresses motor brush noise.

### B. Polling Loop Latency & Debouncer Edge-Misses
- **Problem**: When the motor runs, the cam passes over the switches very quickly (sometimes in $<20\text{ms}$). A standard polling loop (which runs every $1\text{ms}$ to $10\text{ms}$ under FreeRTOS context switching) combined with a $5\text{ms}$ stable-window debouncer (`Bounce2`) would completely filter out the switch press as noise, leading to catastrophic overshoot.
- **Solution**: Implemented hardware-level edge-triggered interrupts (`FALLING` edge) that trigger within microseconds of contact.

### C. Direct Register Cache-Miss Crash (Core 1 Lockup)
- **Problem**: To speed up reads in the Interrupt Service Routines (ISRs), we replaced the Arduino `digitalRead()` function with the native ESP-IDF `gpio_get_level()` function. However, `gpio_get_level()` resides in Flash memory. Calling flash-resident functions from a hardware ISR on Core 1 caused cache-miss lockups, freezing Core 1 (so the motor ran continuously and overshot), while Core 0 (handling WiFi/web server) stayed responsive.
- **Solution**: Reverted to Arduino's `digitalRead()`, which is explicitly compiled into Instruction RAM (IRAM) using the `IRAM_ATTR` attribute, guaranteeing crash-free ISR executions.

### D. Startup Blanking Window vs. Edge-Misses
- **Problem**: Initially, we added a startup blanking window ($30\text{ms}$ or $50\text{ms}$) to ignore inrush current noise. However, when operated in quick succession (where the motor is warm, friction is low, and capacitors are charged), the motor starts extremely fast, hitting the parcel switch *during* the blanking window. Because the interrupts are edge-triggered and the magnet stayed on the switch, the edge was missed, and it overshot.
- **Solution**: Removed the blanking window entirely. Since we have a customized $20\mu\text{s}$ digital filter inside the ISR (`isPinPressedISR()` checking the pin 3 times with $10\mu\text{s}$ delays), startup noise is rejected at the digital level, making the blanking window unnecessary.

### E. Autolock Stutterstep Loop
- **Problem**: When the closed switch triggered, the ISR immediately set `currentState = LOCKED` and braked the motor. However, on the next tick, the autolock loop saw `LOCKED` state but the `Bounce2` debouncer (which is slow) was still reporting `isClosedPressed() == false`. The autolock system assumed the door fell open and immediately re-triggered `LOCKING` state, causing the motor to stutterstep.
- **Solution**: Added a $1\text{-second}$ cooldown to the default autolock checker (`millis() - lockedStateEnterTime > 1000`) and a $2.5\text{-second}$ cooldown to consecutive manual/Wiegand open requests, allowing the hardware, power grid, and debouncer variables to settle cleanly.

### F. Startup Boost vs. Continuous Full-Power Overshoot
- **Problem**: Lower duty cycles might not provide enough torque to overcome initial static friction and move the cam. However, driving the motor continuously at `FULL_POWER_DUTY_CYCLE` (200) causes rapid movement, massive overshoot, and severe mechanical damage/jamming.
- **Solution**: Implemented a ramp profile in the motor controller. It applies `FULL_POWER_DUTY_CYCLE` for a brief boost of exactly `FULL_POWER_MS` (100ms) to overcome static friction, then ramps down to the configured target duty cycle (e.g. `dutyCycleOpen` or `dutyCycleClose`).

## 4. Key Rules for Future Modifications
- **Do not use non-IRAM functions in ISRs**: Any helper function called inside `closedSwitchISR()`, `parcelSwitchISR()`, or `mailSwitchISR()` (such as pin-checking functions) [must](../src/SwitchManager.cpp) have the `IRAM_ATTR` attribute. Never call standard ESP-IDF driver functions unless they are explicitly mapped to IRAM in `sdkconfig`.
- **Do not introduce time-based startup blanking windows**: The travel distance between `Closed` and `Parcel` is mechanically very short. Any time-based masking will cause overshoots on fast runs. Always rely on high-speed pin sampling (`ets_delay_us`) for noise filtering instead.
- **Maintain the Autolock Cooldown**: Always ensure that the autolock logic gives the `Bounce2` debouncer enough time to update its state after an ISR-driven state change.
- **Never drive the motor continuously at FULL_POWER_DUTY_CYCLE**: Always restrict full power (200) to the brief 100ms startup boost (`FULL_POWER_MS`). Operating continuously at full power will cause overshoot and damage the mechanics.
